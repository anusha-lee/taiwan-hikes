Dali Datong Villages A Hike Worth Doing Every Year to See the Paradise in East Taiwan

Posted by:  Anusha Lee
Published date: December 22, 2020
Disclaimer: Please note some of the following are based on my personal condition, and I will try to provide the latest and correct information as possible as I can. Please feel free to let me know if I make any mistake and thank you for reading. Please also note that your safety is your own responsibility.

About Dali and Datong Villages and Shakadang Trail:

Dali 同禮 and Datong Villages 大同部落 are located in the tablelands between Shakadang River and Mt. Qingshui in Sioulin Township, Hualien, and are preserved areas for indigenous people, Truku. Those villages are so remote that they can only be accessed by foot. Therefore, the trail of Dali and Datong is the only one connected to the outside world.
The old name of Dali Village was Huhus (or Xoxos) meaning the sounds made by snakes, and Datong Village was called Skadang, meaning molar. Villagers have to rely on pulley to carry their goods and things for daily life, which costs NT6,000 per trip. Otherwise, they have to carry those things on foot. Life isn’t easy for them, but this simple lifestyle gives them the most stunning nature in return.
Please don’t get confused Shakadang Forestry Road 砂卡礑林道 with Shakadang Trail 砂卡礑步道. The latter one is much easier to hike and can be a standalone trail for small children to enjoy, too. The mountains, Shakadang River and plants on the trail are amazing. The teal color of Shakadang River is so vibrant and mesmerizing and so relaxing by just looking at the water. This trail is so good that I decide to write another post dedicated to it (post coming soon).

How to Get There

Before you go, you need to apply for permit if you want to hike further from Dali and Datong Villages via Sanjianwu 三間屋 to Shakendang Trail, like what I did. You also need to check whether the trails are open for the public to hike. Sometimes, the trails are closed due to the landslide or other reasons.
You can take Taroko Bus 302 from Xincheng Train Station 新城火車站, or Taiwan Tourist Shuttle Taroko Route 1133A from Hualien Train Station 花蓮火車站 to Taroko National Park Visitor Center 太魯閣國家公園遊客中心 from Hualien Train Station. Please note that there’s a Bus 1133 (without A) and it goes to Hualien downtown and takes at least 30 minutes longer to arrive.

Peaks to Reach

Mt. Liwu: 立霧山, 1,274 meters (4,180 feet) also listed as a Xiao Baiyu 小百岳. There’s a Class 3 Triangulation Stone, but no views on the summit. If I do this trail again, I will skip this peak because my goal is to visit Dali and Datong Villages.
Sapah Tadaw Mawna: 達道的家, around 1,100 meters (3,609 feet) is not a peak, but you shouldn’t miss this place. This is a B&B located on a tableland overlooking the most epic valleys and mountains in the east Taiwan. There's hot water for you to take a hot shower, but no hair dryer, because they need the solar power for the essential appliance.
You can choose to share the big bed with other hikers indoors or stay inside a tent provided by them. The cost includes two meals (breakfast and dinner). There's some internet access. I found Sapah Tadaw Mawna Facebook page, but it’s better to make a phone call to book your stay at least 2 weeks in advance.

Start the Hike with Hot, Beautiful Weather and Cramped Legs

GPS coordinates of the trailhead:
We stayed at a hotel in Xinchen, Hualien for one night and headed to Taroko Visitor Center at 7 am. When we arrived at the hotel, it was very hot inside our room. So, we adjusted the air conditioning to a much lower temperature. Then, my legs cramped when I got up.
We arrived at Taroko Visitor Center around 8 pm, and my legs were still cramped. Our guide told me it was probably because of the low temperature during the night. The hot and humid weather didn’t help much, either. But I was still happy to see the gorgeous weather ahead of our hike.
After going to the toilet and getting ready, we took a stair close to the toilet, and this stair connected to Dekalun Trail. You can keep taking the stair trails all the way up, but we took the rugged, because we are hikers, and we enjoy challenges.
Dekalun Trail wasn’t long, around 1.3 km, and we reached a very steep slope. My legs cramped still gave me a hard time, and I had to stop several times to massage them, which made my hike even slower. Our guide told me to take salt to relieve the condition, and it helped a little bit. Luckily, my cramp wasn’t too serious. I've seen a female hiker who suffered a very bad cramp, and she sat on the trail screaming because of the pain.

A Pulley Relay Station with the Amazing View

After some steep slopes, we reached the pulley relay station 流籠中繼站 and took a break. This was such a wonderful spot to take a break. We walked to the edge of the station and we could see Taroko Visitor Center, Liwu River, and the infamous cement factory that was located on the barren hill.
I turned to the mountains to see where the pulley went. It went all the way up to the somewhere on the hilltop. The pulley or cableway used to transport logs. The logging industry had been active for more than 100 years in Taiwan before it came to a halt when government issued the ban of virgin forest logging in 1980s. However, illegal loggings still happen from time to time, and this is another story to tell.
But this pulley still carries on its job to transport the goods for the local people dutifully. I realized later that this pulley was the main lifeline for the villagers to transport goods. Life is still not easy for many people.

Entered Dali Trail to A Flatter Terrain

After the break, we continued our hike. Dekalun Trail ended here, and the trail conditions were slightly better and less steep. Most of the trails from Dekalun and Dali Trails were covered with shade, but the humidity and heat were still too much in summer.
There were signs warning about the risks of wasps and snakes. We didn’t have many typhoons coming to Taiwan in 2020, and many wasp nests remained intact, which led to many hikers being stung on the trails in different parts of Taiwan. It’s definitely not fun to have a close encounter with those tiny worriers.
When we finally reached to the flatter part of Dali Trail, we saw Chongde Automatic Repeater Station 氣象局雨量測報系統崇德中繼站, a machine to collect precipitation information, at least that was what it said on the sign. We took a break here and obviously this part could be accessed by small vehicles. My cramped legs felt better since the hike was not that steep.

The Cutest Abandoned Church with the Most Spectacular Valley View

We hiked a little further and saw a junction with a big and two smaller signs telling us that we were about to enter Dali Tribe, or Huhus, the old name of Dali Tribe in Truku meaning the sound made by snakes or a place with many snakes.
This detour took us down to Dali Church. Before reaching the church, we passed some houses that have people living there and an old, discharged police station. There was a sign explaining the brief history of this area.
Around 1980, Dali villagers moved to Minle Community 民樂社區 near Taroko, and many houses were abandoned ever since. Lucky for us, we still got to see them standing on the hills.
I was intrigued by the history of this village, but I had a hard time to focus on the stories told on the sign, because I couldn’t take my eyes off the surrounding mountains.
We continued to go downhill and saw a sign pointing to our destination. We made one more turn and I almost let out a shriek when I saw it: an unassuming little gray house which was the perfect match to the stunning valley view.
I forgot about the heat and my cramped legs and walked as fast as I could to get close to this tiny church. I left my backpack under a tree and took a walk around the church. It was bigger than I thought, and the front door was locked.
There was a sign saying that hikers could camp here for NT150 per person. I couldn't help but imagine the starry sky I could see at night here. After I was satisfied, I got back to the tree and had my lunch while enjoying the valley and mountains with the fluffy clouds in the bright blue sky.
The views here were so epic that I have to share several photos here.
Later I found some old photos of Daling Village when the tree in front of the church was still a baby. It had all grown up when I visited there in July 2020.

Back to Shakadang Forestry Road to Mt. Liwu

After our lunch break, we took the same trail back to Shakadang Forestry Road and saw a junction before reaching it. The trail from the junction was called Tongli Trail, which could take you to Datong Village.
After we got back on Shakadang Forestry Road, there was a hut covered by plastic tarp and we met a couple cooking their lunch. They were very friendly and told us that we could draw water or wash our faces from the faucet. If you camp at Dali Church, you can come here to draw water, which was around 25 minutes from the church.
Back to the forestry road, we reached a big white cliff wall. There was no plant growing on this cliff wall, and we could see the beautiful white color. We waited here for a few minutes for other hiking mates to catch up, which also gave me a few more minutes to admire this cliff wall.
The mountains in the distance were gorgeous.
Finally, we reached the junction we’d been striving for, the trail to Mt. Liwu, 1,274 meters (4,180 feet). The round trip from the fork to the summit of Mt. Liwu was around 70 minutes, but some of our guides and hikers decided not to go. I knew why later.
I was expecting to see the magnificent views from the top of the summit. The trail was much steeper than Shakadang Forestry Road, the woods was quite dense, and the terrains were very rugged. When I finally got there, I realized that we could barely find enough space to take group photos.
I have to say I was quite disappointed, but this trail still gave me something wonderful to remember. I found Calanthe sylvatica 長距根節蘭, a native orchid in Taiwan, before reaching the summit. Those amazing flowers just stood out of the dense trees, and I had to take many photos of them.

Everything was Paid off and Everything was Perfect

After this, we still had some small hills to climb to get to our destination and stay overnight. I took the last steep hill feeling defeated, but then the things in front of my eyes made me feel like being born again. I forget the heat, my cramped legs, almost everything:
I ran to the edge of the front yard at Sapah Tadaw Mawna 達道的家, our B&B for that night, trying to absorb the views presenting in front of me as much as possible: the mountains, the clouds, the sky, and the light. Everything was perfect!
Our guides told us there was rock platform nearby. I abandoned my backpack and ran over there. Some of our hiking mates were already there. My timing was perfect. There was a beam of sunlight breaking through the clouds and pointing at a small tableland in the distance.
Waiting impatiently for my fellow hikers to finish their photos taking, I could feel my heart was going to explode with joy and gratitude, even though the magic beam of light disappeared within a few minutes.
After spending enough time on the rock, I got back to the house to take a look at the place we were going to stay. You can stay inside the house or at the tents. This was a humble looking place, but I think it was a perfect match to the surrounding mountains. We didn’t need a fancy man-made building to destroy this amazing landscape.
There was a bench on the edge of the yard. I sat there watching the sky changing its colors again. It was getting dark and the setting sun turned the clouds red. I can’t find another word to describe what I saw. It was just perfect.
Needless to say, the starry sky was awaiting us in the evening. My small camera wasn’t able to pick up the stars, but it didn’t matter because I was there.

The Most Astonishing Breakfast I’ve Ever Had

We got up very early to catch the sunrise. Although the weather was cloudy, we still managed to see some stunning colors from the sky.
But when we were on our way back to the house, we saw Mt. Sanjiaozhui 三角錐山, 2,607 meters (8,553 feet), bathing in the early sun light, which gave us a totally different perspective from what we saw from yesterday.
Not just this, by the time we got back, we were lucky enough to take the best table to have the breakfast. Our breakfast was tasteful. The owners of this house carried all the ingredients up there, which made the food taste even more special. The view in front of us made me feel humble even more.
The name of this place belonged to the tribal chief, Tadaw Grandpa 達道爺爺, but sadly he passed away in 2018 at the age of 83. His son and daughter in law take over this place to provide a temporary home for hikers. They were as friendly as Tadaw Grandpa, and they could tell you all the peaks you saw there. But my short memories couldn’t remember all of them.
Before we left, we explored this area again and took even more photos. I felt sad to leave here so soon, but I promised myself that I will come back again.

Sakadan Village like a Fairy Tale

We got back to Shakadang Forestry Road again. The trail was pleasant and flat to hike. We reached a junction with a sign saying Sakadan Village 砂卡礑部落 after 50 minutes.
We left our backpacks here and took that direction to Sakadan Village. We passed through beautiful bamboo woods and the landscape opened up. We arrived at Sakadan Village.
This village was located in the valley and it looked like it was hugged by the lovely mountains.
I saw a big red sign pointing to the direction to 千里眼山 Mt. Qianliyan. Our guide told us that this was the trail to Mt. Qianliyan and 清水大山 Big Mt. Qingshui.
I took a short walk and decided to get up to the grass hill. I sat down, watching the mountains and clouds in the distance. It was for sure that this place would never disappoint you. You can also book your stay here.

Started the Challenging Hike but an Unfortunate Accident Happened

After the happy detour to visit Sakadan Village, we got back to the trail. From there, the trail terrains become rugged again and there were many rocky slopes. Our guides reminded us to take our time and there was no need to be in a hurry.
Just after we passed the second big rocky slope, a female hiker who was a few meters behind me and was still on the rocky slope suddenly sat down. Then, she spitted out the thing that was every hiker’s nightmare: I think I just broke my leg.
Our guides rushed to her side to check her condition. When one female guide tried to touch her leg to access her condition, the injured hiker yelped in pain. I couldn’t see much from the place I stood, and we waited there patiently. After discussion, our guides decided to call 112 for help. I think we waited there at least one hour.
Two guides stayed with the injured hiker waiting for the rescue team to come. The other two guides took us back to our hired bus. Everyone was still in shock and kept reminding each other to be careful. There were still some rocky and steep hills to pass, and we all tried to stay alert as much as we could.
Then, we reached Sanjianwu 三間屋, which was the end of the steep hike and we had our lunch break. While having our food, I saw the first arrival of two rescue team members riding their scooters. Although the trail was flat after Sanjianwu, the uneven trail terrain still made their scooter riding difficult, which means the rest of the team had to get to there on foot.
After lunch break, we still had 4.1 km to reach the parking lot at Taroko Visitor Center. Now, we were on Sakadang Trail, not the challenging forestry road.
If it weren’t the accident, Shakadang Trail would be very a very beautiful trail and pleasant to hike. But we were still in a somber mood because of the accident. Long before we reached the parking lot, it started to rain, and I was soaking wet. Compared to our injured mate, being soaking wet seemed not big deal. At least, we made it back to our bus safely.

Some Thoughts about this Trail: A Trail worth to Visit Every Year

This is a truly amazing trail to hike and visiting Sapah Tadaw Mawna again and again is definitely worth to do every year.
There are many options to enjoy your stay in this place. 1. Stay in Hualien for one night and get to Taroko Visitor Center to start your hike and stay at Sapah Tadaw Mawna for another night. If I have enough time, I’d love to stay there at least for 2 nights.
2. Stay one night at Dali Church for one night and head to Sapah Tadaw Mawna for another. Please remember to contact and pay in advance for your stay at Dali Church. But you will need to carry your own tent and sleeping bag.
3. Spend your second night at Sapah Tadaw Mawna and your third at Sakadang Village. There are some B&B like Rainbow House 彩虹屋 to stay.
As to how to get back to Taroko Visitor Center, I will take the same trail back to the parking lot instead of making a loop hike, and you don’t have to apply for a permit if you do so. The trail condition isn’t as good as the previous section from Sapah Tadaw Mawna to Sanjianwu, if you are not familiar with the trail terrains in Taiwan. Plus, this section is often closed.
There are many hiking trails in Taiwan, but I haven’t got a chance to really explore those remote ones in Hualien and Taitung. After Dali Datong Villages, my goal is to hike Shakadang Tree Heros 砂卡礑三雄: Big Mt. Qingshui 清水大山 2,408 meters (7,900 feet), Mt. Liwu 立霧山, 1,274 meters (4,180 feet), Mt. Qianliyan 千里眼山, 1,624 meters (5,828 feet). Hopefully, I will get to do this soon.
If you prefer a more relaxing hike, Shakandang Trail is for you. I’ve been this trail several times, and I can always find something new on this trail. For more details about Shakandang Trail, click here for more details.
