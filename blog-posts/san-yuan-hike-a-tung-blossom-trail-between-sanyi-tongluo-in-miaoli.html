About San-Yuan Hike, Sanyi and Tongluo Townships

This is a ridgeline hike starting from Sanyi to Tongluo via Mt. Sanjiao, Mt. Changkeng, Mt. Shuangfeng, Mt. Shuangfeng North Peak, and ending with Mt. Yuantun. To make it easier to remember, hikers take the first word of the first and last mountains and call it San-Yuan Hike.

Sanyi and Tongluo

60% of people in Miaoli and 15% of the total population in Taiwan are Hakka people or Hakka Han 客家, literally meaning Guest Families in Chinese. Hakka Han are a Chinese subgroup and came from southern China provinces. Hakka people have their own language, distinct culture, architecture and foods. In Sanyi and Tongluo, 94% of the population in each township is Hakka people. If you have been to Taipei and take MRT, you must have heard of Hakka language because the voice announcements include Mandarin, Taiwanese, Hakka and English.
Sanyi is famous for her wood sculpture making because of many camphor trees in the old days, and one of the 4 townships in Taiwan certified as “Slow City” by Cittaslow. Actually, Miaoli has two townships listed, and the other one is Nanzhuang.
There are several tourist attractions in Sanyi, and some of the famous ones are Longten Broken Bridge 龍騰斷橋 and Mt. Huoyan or Huoyanshan 火炎山.
Mt. Huoyan is famous for her spectacular landscape that consists of conglomerate and sandstone layers, and later was designated as a nature reserve area. There’s a hiking trail to Mt. Huoyan, but there are several accidents near the cliff because of the fragile geological structure. Please DO NOT get too close to the cliffs when you are trying to take photos.
Like Sanyi, Tongluo is a famous Hakka town, and there are many delicious Hakka restaurants in Tongluo. Mt. Shuangfeng is the highest mountain in Tongluo. If you want to know more about Hakka history, you can visit of Hakka Yard 客家大院 if you get there.

San-Yuan Hike: A Relaxing Ridgeline Trail

You can also start from the Tongluo side, and the trail is much easier than the Sanyi side. Just follow the steps and you’ll reach Mt. Yuantun. The other trailhead near Mt. Yuantun is from the back of Hakka Yard 客家大院, but this one was closed when I went there.
If you enjoy tung blossoms, you can’t miss this trail in April and May. San-Yuan Trail is one of the famous places to see the snow-like blossoms in Miaoli.
Other than tung blossoms, this is also a ridgeline hike, and you can see the second highest mountain, Mt. Xue Main Peak (aka Mt. Snow, Mt. Sylvia), height 3,886 meters, in Taiwan. This trail terrain isn’t too rugged or complicated. Occasionally you’ll have to walk through some narrow ridges before reaching Mt. Shuangfeng. It’s a point-to-point hike and both sides can be accessed by train.
Mt. Shuangfeng is listed as one of Miaoli Eight Scenes 苗栗八景. During Japanese Occupation, it was said the shape of Mt. Shuangfeng looked like Mt. Fuji. Therefore, it was also called Miaoli’s Mt. Juji. I didn’t get to see the shape of Mt. Shuangfen from the distance, so I can’t comment whether it really looks like Mt. Fuji.
You don’t have to go through the whole trail. The new friends I made on my second trip were planning to turn back after reaching Mt. Sanjiao, which can be reached within 30 minutes.

How to Get There

You can take Local Train and get off at Sanyi or Tongluo Train Station. If you want to drive a car or ride your scooter to either train station, you can park there and take the train back to your car or scooter. It takes only 7 minutes from Sanyi to Tongluo or Tongluo to Sanyi by train.

Peaks and Places to Reach

Lantern Earth God Temple: 燈籠福德祠, height 336 meters (1,102 feet). This is also the entrance of Mt. Sanjiao behind the parking lot. One of the big trees behind this temple is camphor tree 大樟公 and is said to be over 100 years old.
Mt. Sanjiao: 三角山, height 567 meters (1,860 feet). There’s a Class 3 Triangulation Stone, not much to see on the peak.
Mt. Changkeng aka Mt. Sanjiao North Peak: 長坑山 or 三角山北峰, height 560 meters (1,837 feet). You can see Provincial Highway 13 台13線 and Tongluo Industrial Park.
Mt. Shuangfeng: 雙峰山, height 538 meters (1,765 feet), the highest mountain in Tongluo.
Mt. Shuangfeng North Peak: 雙峰山北峰, height 450 meters (1,476 feet).
Mt. Yuantun: height 339 meters (1,112 feet), a Class 3 Triangulation Stone.

Before We Start: Same Trail on Different Times and Seeing Different Things

On my first hike in February 2020, my friends and I started from Sanyi Train Station, reached Mt. Sanjiao, Mt. Changkeng, and Mt. Shuangfeng, and then we took the turn to Cihui Temple at a fork to have a lunch break. From there, I forgot to turn back to the fork, took a wrong turn and we ended up getting off the trail. We had to walk back to Sanyi Train Station and one of our friends drove us around to see the tourist attractions nearby. That's how I got the photos of the ridgeline from the distance.
On my second hike in October 2020, I met a couple and we started hiking after Mt. Changkeng and had lunch together near Mt. Shuangfeng. For some unknown reason, I didn’t get to Mt. Shuangfeng or see the fork to Cihui Temple.
This is a point-to-point hike and can be done in 5 hours. I started from Sanyi Train Station for my both attempts. It took me one hour from my place to Taipei Train Station, and 2 hours and 44 minutes to Sanyi Train Station from Taipei. It had been raining a lot in northern Taiwan in 2020, and it was no exception when I left home in New Teipei City on that morning. When the train arrived at Sanyi, I was excited to see the sunny blue sky.
As usual, having a map or GPX track with you is a must, especially when you need to get to the trailhead from Sanyi Train Station. There are many marks on the way to the trailhead, but they are all in Chinese. I turned right after leaving the train station and saw a big sign pointing to Mt. Sanjiao next to an underpass on my right. So, I turned right again.
You'll have to make several turns before reaching the trailhead of Mt. Sanjiao, but, lucky for hikers, there are many signs and a map telling us where to turn.
After 10+ minutes, I arrived a parking lot and the trailhead was at the other end.

Start with A Cute Earth God Temple with A Cute Name and Quicky to Mt. Sanjiao

There was an Earth God Temple above the parking lot, called Denglong Temple 燈籠福德祠, or Lantern Temple. I didn’t see this on my first hike. I’m thankful that my new friends took me there and they also pointed the toilet to me. There were two big trees that were over 100 years old behind the temple. You can go there to take a look before your hike.
The trail was obvious, endless steps, and some of the steps were made of sandbags, which could be very slippery if those got wet.
I just followed them and stopped once in a while to see the views.
Then I reached a very steep slope with ropes to help hikers getting up or down. I’d say this was the steepest slope on this hike, but it was over within a few minutes.
Following the trail, I saw a hue power tower on my left and I continued. After making a turn, I reached the first peak, Mt. Sanjiao. There was a big space on this peak, but not much view so I quickly left.

Layers of Mt Xue Range Kept Me Company to Mt. Changkeng with New Friends

From there, I started to hike on the ridges. On my right, I could see layers of mountains. Those are sections of the famous Mt. Xue Range 雪山山脈, and this range goes all the way to the easternmost Taiwan, Sandiaojiao.
The ridgeline hike was up and down and up and down, but there wasn’t many huge ascending or descending.
I met a couple when I reached Mt. Sanjiao, and we ran into each other again several times on this trail. Their Plan A was to hike to Mt. Sanjiao, enjoy their time there and go home. After having a conversation with me, they decided to join me to do this point-to-point hike instead.
Sometimes, there were several narrow ridges to pass through, and some small steep slopes to climb up. Other than those, this hike wasn’t difficult at all.
Very soon, I reached Mt. Changkeng, aka Mt. Sanjiao North Peak. There was an opening on the west side of this ridgeline, and I could see Tongluo in the distance.
If you like Taiwan Hikes, feel free to buy me a coffee. Thank you very much for your support!

To Mt. Shuangfeng, Supposedly

After Mt. Mt. Changkeng, there was a slope with a flimsy looking ladder hanging on another steep slope. It looked a little bit dangerous, but it wasn’t too bad.
Then I passed a power tower.
I was wondering whether the tower was the same one I saw from Provincial Highway 13 after making the wrong turn on my previous hike.
On my first hike on February 8, 2020, my friends and I reached Mt. Shuangfeng right after climbing the flimsy ladder and saw a 3-way fork, one to Cihui Temple and the other to Shuangfeng Road.
We went to Cihui Temple to have lunch and totally forgot to get back here. My first hike ended with Mt. Shuangfeng. Hikers say there is a big power tower near Mt. Shuangfeng, but I can’t remember whether I’d seen it.
On my second hike, my new friends and I saw a small platform like cliff with a beautiful view, and we decided to have a lunch break to enjoy the scenery together.
However, I didn’t reach Mt. Shuangfeng on my second hike, nor did I see the 3-way fork. I check other hikers’ blogs and it seems the local government set up new signs. This is still a mystery to me and can only be solved by a third attempt.

Mt. Yuantun North Peak and Finally, Mt. Yuantun

After lunch break on my second hike, my friends and I continued. The trail terrain became much milder, and we arrived at Mt. Yuantun North Peak within a few minutes.
The trail was well maintained, and I even saw a stone carving with millage on it, 0K 495M. I guessed that was the starting point from the other side of the trail and the altitude.
Looing through the trees, I still could get glimpses of mountains from the east.
Then, we reached our last mountain peak on this trail, Mt. Yuantun. There was much to see here, either. So, we continued our hike.
From here, the trail was even much easier, mostly steps.
We passed a fence with three signs, the one on the right pointing to Hakka Yard, one pointing to Mt. Yuantun, and the third one pointing to Tongluo Train Station. We continued to the train station.
Within a few minutes, we exited the trail, and my new friends arranged a pickup for us to get back to Sanyi Train Station.
The distance from the trailhead to Tongluo Train Station is 2.2 km (1.4 miles) if you walk on Taiwan County Highway 119, and pass Tongluo Fire-cracker Vine Flower Park 銅鑼炮仗花海公園. The blossom season of Fire-cracker Vine is in February and March, which is about the time of cherry blossoms and also available in the park.

Bonuses from Both Trips: Cherry Trees Trail in Yuanli and Beautiful Sunset on Mt. Hutou in Tongxiao

Although I didn’t get to finish the whole hike on my first trip, we drove to nearby attractions to see Cherry Trees Trail in Yuanli Township and got to see the ridgeline we hiked earlier.
We saw Taiwan High Speed Railway trains passed by, too.
As to my second trip, my new friends were so warm and friendly. As matter of fact, most Taiwanese hikers are very friendly and willing to help hikers in need. My new friends arranged a pickup, and their friend, Mt. Lee, took us to his cabin to have snacks and Chinese tea. Mr. Lee was a retiree, but he had a very busy and colorful life. He let us visit his cabin in a remote place in Miaoli, and it was a great getaway to enjoy the country life.
Later, my new friends took me to Mt. Hutou Trail 虎頭山步道 and enjoyed sunset on Mt. Hutou 虎頭山 in Tongxiao. This is probably the shortest hiking trail I’ve ever been, only 600 meters round trip. Hutou literally means tiger’s head in Chinese.
The height of Mt. Hutou is only 93 meters, more like a small hill, but you can see the panoramic view of whole area.
There is one Class 2 Triangulation Stone and a Class 3 one, but I only saw the class 2 one. There was a Tongxiao Shrine 通宵神社, but it was already closed when we got there.
By the way, there are 5 mountains with the name of Hutou in Taiwan, and the locations of the other four are: Guishan District of Taoyuan City, Puli Township of Nantou County, Kenting (Checheng Township) of Pingtung County, and Tuisui Township of Hualien County. The one in Kenting is actually called Mt. Dinghutou 頂虎頭山. Ding means top in Chinese.
Later, my friends took me to Tongxiao Train Station to call it a day.
