About Syakaro Historic Trail: The First National Trail in Taiwan

Syakaro or Xiakelo Historic Trail (I’ll use Syakaro on this post) is a trail between Wufeng Township and Jianshi Township in Hsinchu County, Taiwan. It is also the first National Trail finished and open to the public in 2003 and attracted so many hikers to witness the beauty and history of Syakaro. However, when Typhoon Mindulle struck Taiwan in summer 2004, the floods and landslides destroyed this trail and Syakaro was closed again.
It took years for the government to rebuild the trail which has been battered by typhoons so many times. The trail was closed and open to the public on and on for years. In December 2018, it was finally open to the public again and becomes one of the most popular historic trails in Taiwan.
Syakaro means Formosan Michelia 烏心石 in Atayal language, and there were a lot of these trees in the upstream of Touqian River 頭前溪 around this area. Around 250 years ago, a group of Atayal people moved here, and this tribe was named after Syakaro.

A Trail with the Highest Outpost Density to Control Atayal People

According to the author of A Story of the Xiakalo Historic Trail, Xu Rulin, the total length of Syakaro was 49.2 km (30.6 miles), but the north and south sections of the trail were transformed into modern roads. So, the left Syakaro Historic Trail is only 22 km (13.7 miles) now.
Like Zhuilu Old Trail, Syakaro Historic Trail was also a trail paved by bloods. During Japanese Occupation, the Japanese government used this trail to control the indigenous people, Atayal, especially after two Syakaro Incidents 霞喀羅事件. According to Dr. Lee Ruizong 李瑞宗, there were 25 outposts build on the trail to supervise and control Atayal People, almost one outpost every 2 km, and Syakaro had the highest outpost density among the trails during that time.

From Syakaro Trail to Mt. Dabajian and the Holy Ridgeline

In August 1927, a group of Japanese with their indigenous Atayal guides started from Syakaro Trail, took several Cautionary Paths/Security Paths (this is the translation I saw on the signs, but I think it means Security Paths) 警備道路 and reached Madala River Trailhead 馬達拉溪登山口, which is the trailhead to Mt. Dabajian 大霸尖山 today.
On August 4, 1927, seven Japanese successfully reached the top of Mt. Dabajian (which is not allowed now), not just the base of Mt. Dabajian. However, the Mt. Dabajian was deemed as a sacred mountain in Atayal culture, those Atayal guides only stayed at the base.
When one of the Japanese hikers, Numai Tetsutaro 沼井鐵太郎, reached Mt. Yizhe 伊澤山 3,297 meters (10,817 feet) and looked at the spectacular ridgeline from Mt. Xue to Mt. Dabajian, he acclaimed this ridgeline as Holy Ridgeline (aka Shengleng Trail) 聖稜線. This is where the Holy Ridgeline came from and climbing the Holy Ridgeline has become hikers’ dreams ever since.

How to Get There and How to Plan Your Hike

There is no public transport to get to either trailhead. Since it’s a point-to-point trail, it’s difficult to drive your car to get there. No buses are allowed to get to the trailheads, either. You either hire a van with a driver to take you to one of the trailheads and wait for you to finish at the other or have a group of friends driving two cars and hike from both ends and exchange the car keys in the middle.

Do the Hike in One Day

As to how many days it should take to finish this hike, it depends on your physical fitness and your preference. If you are fit but don’t want to carry too much weight, you can do it in one day.

Do the Hike in Two Days and Carry Your Own Things

If you are fit but want to take your time to enjoy this trail, you can do what I did and plan a two-day trip. Most hikers will camp at Baishih Outpost 白石駐在所, and you will find toilets there. Please remember to bring toilet papers with you, and camp near the old building. It’s not allowed to camp inside the building. But as you can imagine, it’s very crowded at Baishih Outpost and it was like a tent city when I was there.

Do the Hike in Two Days BUT Hire Porters to Carry Things for You

If you don’t want to carry your own food, tents, and sleeping bags, you can hire porters to help you.

Day 1: A Trail with Many Beautiful Suspension Bridges

GPS coordinates of Shilu Trailhead: 24.561246, 121.169258
I joined Dudu 嘟嘟 for this hike. Actually, this was my second time to hike this trail, but I didn’t finish the whole trail in December 2018. I always wanted to do the whole trail and I’m glad I finally made it 2 years later.
We started from Shilu Trailhead 石鹿登山口 near Qingquan 清泉 at 10:46 am. If you want to do the whole trail, Shilu Trailhead is usually the one you start because you will hike downhill mostly from here. You’ll find many signs and maps telling you the history and information you need to know on this trail.
If you need to go to toilet, there is one at 1.2 km.
The trail terrain was quite flat and easy to hike. We were accompanied with tall slander Japanese Cedar Trees, and it was quite relaxing to walk there. It was a little bit cloudy when we started the hike, but we still could see beams of sunlight seeping into the woods.
This trail is well marked, and you’ll find the millage marks in increments of one kilometer.

Beautiful Fall Foliage and Sea of Clouds

Fall is the high season to hike Syakaro Historic Trail because of fall foliage. I didn’t get to see much but it was still a great delight to see some bright red leaves dancing in between trees.
Because we started the hike late, we stopped soon to take a lunch break. I took a spot facing the cliff. At first, all I could see was trees and clouds. Suddenly, I saw parts of blue sky appearing in the distance. I put down my lunch and rushed to the edge of the slope and waited. These are what I saw.
After lunch break, we continued to hike. Our guides told us that there was a bypass to avoid the dangerous ridge section. I saw ropes circled the dangerous area, and there was sea of clouds! I reached the rope area, carefully not to step on the loose slope and took photos.
This has reminded me again and again that things might be much different when you break through certain heights on the trails, or in your life.
After the ridgeline, we were back to the woods again. Inside the woods, the mist floated on the trail, which made the trail look mystical.
