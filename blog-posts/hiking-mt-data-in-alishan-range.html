Visit the highest Peak on One of the Most Famous Tourist Attractions in Taiwan: Hiking Mt. Data in Alishan Range

Posted by:  Anusha Lee
Disclaimer: Please note some of the following are based on my personal condition, and I will try to provide the latest and correct information as possible as I can. Please feel free to let me know if I make any mistake and thank you for reading. Please also note that your safety is your own responsibility.

How to Get There

Since Mt. Ali is a popular destination, there are many options to get there. Please click here for more details.
Note: This is a two-day hike in Mt. Ali area, but you can do those trails separately. Please click here to see my first day hike. Like Tefuye Old Trail and Mt. Zhizhong, you can also decide to take the difficult part or the easy one.

Peaks to Reach

Mt. Data: 大塔山 Data Shan, 2,663m (8,737 ft), is the sacred mountain of Taiwanese indigenous people, Tsou. Mt. Data is also the highest one in Mt. Ali range, and listed in Xiao Baiyue in Taiwan.
Mt. Duigaoyue: 對高岳 Duigaoyue, 2,444 m (8,018 ft). No views.
Mt. Zhu: 祝山 Zhu Shan, 2,430 m (7,972 ft).
Mt. Xiaoliyuan: 小笠原山 Xiaoliyuan Shan, 2,488 m (8,162 ft).

A Challenging Start to Wake You Up

Trailhead GPX: 25.503209, 120.821901
Our group stayed at a hotel in Alishan after our hike to Tefuye. One of the things to do in Alishan is to watch the beautiful sunrise, but none of us intended to get up at 4 am, so we got an extra hour to sleep. If you want to see the sunrise and sea of clouds, you can take Zhushan Line Train to Mt. Zhu or Zhushan.
The trailhead (GPX: 25.503209, 120.821901) locates on the side of Zhushan Road. We took the steep slope to a small observation deck to see Mt. Yu peaks. You can also take the Zhushan Road to get there. When we got there, the sun was already up. We didn’t see the sunrise, but we got to see Mt. Yu Peaks (the main peak is 3,952m / 12,966 ft). If you stand at the right spot, you can get to see the total 11 ones.
Our guide told us that we were on the first layer of the mountains on our first day of hike. Looking at the layers of mountains with different shades of blue was truly unique and you will remember it for a very long time.
After the smaller deck, we walked around 15 minutes to reach Mt. Xiaoliyuan Lookout 小笠原山觀景台, and stayed here for a long time to take photos. This lookout was huge. I could imagine how packed it would be for people waiting here to see the stunning sunrise. The views were still gorgeous even without the sunrise. We stayed here for a little bit longer and took as many photos as we could.
The next stop was Mt. Zhu or Zhu Shan 祝山, and this was also a very popular tourist attraction to see the sunrise and sea of clouds. There was a shutter bus stop and Alishan Forest Railway station located at Mt. Zhu.
I remember many years ago my friends and I took this train in the early morning to catch up the first glow of sunrise. At that moment, I didn’t know I was watching Mt. Yu. It was too dark and crowded to pay attention to the sign, too. Now, I was standing at the same place again and got to know more about mountains in Taiwan. Looking at Mt. Yu and other famous peaks on the ridgeline, I was in awe.
To get to our first peak today, we walked along the railway track heading to our next destination, Mt. Duigaoyue 對高岳 (2,444 m, 8,018 ft). It was not crowded at that time and we saw monkeys watching us in the distance. It took us around 30 minutes from Mt. Xiaoliyuan Lookout to reach the trailhead of Mt. Duigaoyue.
The hike was very easy and felt like aimless roaming before we started taking the trail to Mt. Duigaoyue. The trail to Mt. Duigaoyue was not long, around 3 km one way, and all we had to do was just following the endless wooden steps to reach the top. But if you are like me who is not good at taking steps, this trail might still make you huffing and puffing.
Although the steps were a little bit discouraging, it still felt wonderful to walk in the woods, watching the morning sun seeping through the dense woods mixed with Taiwan Red Cypress and Japanese Cedar.
There used to have a shrine build on the top of Mt. Duigaoyue during Japanese occupation years ago. Now the shrine was gone and was replaced by a two-level pavilion. There wasn’t much views on the second level because this structure was surrounded by trees when we arrived there, but I still manage to get a glimpse of Mt. Yu in between leaves.
It took us around one and half an hours to finish this trail. After exiting the trail, we hiked along the railway track again and got to the trailhead of Mt. Data. A very popular trail, 眠月線 Mianyue Xian Trail (permit required) wasn’t far from here, but that wasn’t on our itinerary this time. We took lunch and got some rest at the big deck next to the trailhead to Mt. Data 大塔山.
The trail to Mt. Data once again started with endless steps. If you stay in Taiwan long enough, you will notice that many trails located in tourist attractions are made of steps, either stone ones or wooden ones or both. The longer I hike the more I distaste steps. It seems I'm not alone. Most hikers prefer to hike on the natural trails no matter how difficult it can be, but what’s done is done. I sucked it up and continued the hike. To make my life easier, I decided to hide my big backpack in the bush and just carried the essential things with me to the top since this was a small loop trail.
The trees along the wooden steps were wonderful. There were probably not many old-growth forests left here because Mt. Ali used to be the main supply for logging for decades. However, I hadn’t totally recovered from our previous hike, and I felt exhausted already. One of our guides, Fion, still stayed with me as much as she could.
Finally, we got to the last section of the concrete steps to reach the summit of Mt. Data, 2,663 m (8,737 ft). After seeing the views on the summit, the effort was totally worth it. I saw Mt. Data on my right and Mt. Yu peaks on the left. If you look down, the whole Alishan Forest Recreation Area was in front of you.
In fact, we were not on the summit of Mt. Data. The top deck was close to the real summit. The real summit of Mt. Data was formed by rocks and it was deemed sacred by one of Taiwan indigenous tribes, the Tsou people. It's not allowed to climb to the rocks now.
As to the view to Mt. Yu, it was difficult to get beautiful pictures without showing a phased out military building, but Mt. Yu peaks were still magnificent. We were very lucky to have a great weather two days in a row and I was happy (but still tired).
After spending some time admiring the views, it was time to leave. Climbing up those steps wasn't easy, but it doesn’t mean it would be easier to descend. I could hear my knees complaining out loud when taking the same route back. The rest of our trip was the relatively easy.
We were back to the trail and it took us a while to get to the location to enter the main Alishan tourist trail. This was a very pleasant hike. The trees around us were tall and straight, quite different from the bushes or jungles we used to see on the trails in Taiwan.
Then we reached Sister Ponds, surrounded by magnificent Taiwan Red Cypress. There was a pavilion in each pond, and it was not surprised that many tourists loved this area because it was so beautiful. Again, we stayed there a little bit longer to take pictures before heading back to our hired bus.
On the way to our bus, we passed the most famous temple in Alishan, Shouzhen Temple 受鎮宮, which is also famous for moths to stay on the beard of the gods, but I didn’t go inside. There are some shops and restaurants here. For us, it was about the time to call it a day.

Some Thoughts about This Hike

Alisha was one of the three major logging centers in Taiwan during Japanese occupation. The biggest and most beautiful trees were logged and transported to Japan during that period, and later the ruling part of Taiwan, KMT, after Japanese left hacked down even more trees. Lucky for the land and us, the legal logging industry is a thing in the past, but illegal logging still happens from time to time.
If you get a chance, please take Alishan Forest Railway, which has been operating for more than 100 years with the unique Z-shaped switchbacks to adapt the topology. Here's a great post from Foreigners In Taiwan to teach you how to take the train from Taipei.
There are so many things you can enjoy at Alishan Forest Recreation Area without going through the difficult trails, and you can find plenty of information on the official website or just Googling. There are other tails to explore around Alishan, like Mianyue Xian 眠月線 and Axi Long Distance Hike 阿溪縱走, and I’m planning to hike those trails someday.
