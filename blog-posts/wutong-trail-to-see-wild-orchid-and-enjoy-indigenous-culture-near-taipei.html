WuTong Long Distance: A Trail to See Exotic Wild Orchids and Beautiful Woods in an Indigenous Village near Taipei

Posted by:  Anusha Lee
Published date: November 3, 2020
Disclaimer: Please note some of the following are based on my personal condition, and I will try to provide the latest and correct information as possible as I can. Please feel free to let me know if I make any mistake and thank you for reading. Please also note that your safety is your own responsibility.

About Wulai: A Place with Rich Nature and Indigenous Culture

Wulai 烏來, or Ulay in Atayal, meaning water is hot, is the home of indigenous tribe, Atayal, in New Taipei City, Taiwan. Wulai is also the largest but the least populated district in New Taipei City. Compared Wulai’s population density, 20/square km, to Yuanhe District’s 永和區 30,000/square km. Wulai is a popular tourist attraction, but also a hiker’s heaven. However, the devastating aftermath brought by Typhoon Soudelor in 2015 made Wulai suffer for a long time.
Typhoon Soudelor brought floods in Yilan and Wulai. It produced vast amount of 422 mm / 16.61 inches, within 6 hours in Wulai. The intensity of Soudelor also triggered landslide in the mountains, and the impact lasted for years, which also made me think twice to hike here.

How to Get There

Take Bus 849 at MRT Xingdian Station. If you want to start your hike from Miaoxin Temple 妙心寺, get off at the last stop, 烏來總站 Wulai Terminal.
If you want to visit Wulai Old Street right after your hike without taking the bus back to Wulai Terminal, you get off at Zhongzhi Stop 忠治 and walk slightly uphill for around 45 minutes to Mt. Datong Trail 大桶山登山步道. Please note that it might be more difficult later if you start from this side.

Peaks to Reach

Mt. Wulai Front Peak: 烏來山前峰, 789 meters / 2,588 feet
Mt. Wulai: 烏來山, 820 meters / 2,690 feet
Mt. Datong: 大桶山, 916 meters / 3,005 feet, Class 3 Triangulation Stone, no view
Mt. Datong East Peak: 大桶山東峰, East Peak, 910 meters / 2,985 feet, no view

My Plan B Became Plan A Because of the Virus

Trailhead GPX from Miaoxin Temple 妙心寺: 24.865556, 121.551944
Trailhead GPX from Zhongzhi 忠治, the entrance of Mt. Datong Trail 大桶山登山步道: 24.89037, 121.5585
My Plan A for my birthday was to hike Mt. Jiali 加里山, because I was hoping I could find 一葉蘭 and 水晶蘭. However, the outbreak of Covid-19 messed up many things and I wasn’t sure it was okay to travel there in April. So, I decided to pick up a trail that required me to travel less by public transport. Then, I came across this trail, which also gave me one of the most pleasant surprises.
This trail is a point to-point-one, and you can go either way. I started this hike from the steeper one, which was very close to 烏來老街 Wulai Old Street. The other one was near Zhongzhi Village 忠治部落.
You have to look for the trailhead on your left carefully, because I missed it at first and walked all the way into Miaoxin Temple, while my GPS watch showed that I was further away from the trailhead.

A Steep Beginning to Wake You Up

The trail terrains were typical in Taiwan: soil intertwined with tree roots and rocks, but it wasn’t difficult to navigate. There were also many plastic ribbons tied on the trees to help me follow the trail, and I saw a stair leading up to a huge water tank on my right. Quickly it went uphill. While I was working hard to climb up, I saw Wulai and River Nanshi through the tree branches.
The more I went, the steeper the trail became. Some slopes were almost vertical, but it wasn’t too hard to get myself up because there were many strong tree roots for me to grab.

Auntie, You Are Really Good 阿姨好厲害

To my delight, there weren’t many hikers on the trail. The first people I met were the residents living in Wulai, a guy and two teenage boys doing a short hike. When the guy learned that I went solo, he told the boys that Auntie (which is me) was really good 阿姨好厲害.
Although our group hired porters to carry our sleeping bags, tents and food, I still carried my own sleeping bag and tent. The humid and hot weather made the hike even tougher. But I think I was still in a better shape compared to 10 years ago.
Aside from the steamy weather and rugged terrains, the threes here were tall and amazing. There were also many huge trees, but we also saw what illegal logging has done to the beautiful woods.
We passed many hikers who were heading back to the trailhead. When they saw us, they told us many hikers had arrived at Lake Songluo and to expect a huge crowd. My heart sank when hearing this. I know Lake Songluo is a popular hiking trail, but the crowd did put me off. During pandemic, people can’t travel abroad and many of them flood to the trails.

Finally, the Lake

After the rocky slope, we reached a small platform-like fork. Our guide told us there was a trail to Mt. Quantoumu, (1,551 meters / 5,088 feet), and he could wait for us if any of us wanted to go there. Nope, I said to myself, perhaps next time.
We stayed at the fork for a while waiting for the rest of our group to arrive. From now on, no more ascending, only descending. The vegetation looked different now, and there were more ferns and Songluo/Usnea 松羅 covered on the trees, which made the landscape look mysterious.
Then, I heard our hiking mates’ happy shrieking: the lake! Finally.

Not Much Water Left but Still Amazing

It was cloudy when we arrived at the lake. By the time I finished pitching my tent, it started drizzling. I sat inside my tent, looking at the lake. There was a mist floating on the top of the lake, and It would be very calming and mesmerizing if other hikers could be quieter and contain their excitement.
After a while, the drizzle became a rain shower. I was happy that I brought my own tent, and luckily my neighbor was also a quiet guy. He was very nice to offer me some hot drink and we talked for a while and enjoyed the scenery.
After the rain died down, we decided to explore around this valley. The lake, to be exact, wasn’t a big lake, and was more like several sporadic ponds. When you walked on the grass/moss, it felt like you were walking on sponges, soft and squishy. However, you might step into water because the moss covered the surface of those ponds and you just couldn’t see the water.
After a while, we had to get back to the kitchen tent and queued up for dinner. Our group hired porters to prepare our tents and food. If we didn’t get there sooner, it would take much longer to finish our dinner.

The Amazing Ecology

After dinner, my new friend and I decided to explore this area again. Before I came here, I did some research and many articles mentioned that Lake Songluo is home to 白腹游蛇 Eastern Water Snakes. I was hoping to see some. To my delight, I saw many of them. I even witnessed a battle between an Eastern Water Snake and a frog. The poor frog failed this battle and succumbed to its predator.

Majestic Morning Lights Drifting over Lake Songluo

We got up around 5:30 am the next morning. I didn’t sleep well because of many noisy hikers. I decided to get up and walk around the lake again. At first, it was still cloudy, but soon the sun broke through the sky.
Not long, the sky was cleared up and mixed with light clouds floating by. When I looked at the direction where we entered the lake area yesterday, I saw the sunlight was divided into beams by the peak of a small hill and splashing on the lake. Totally in awe.
Now, I finally understood why Lake Songluo was dubbed dearly as the Lake of 17-year-old Young Maiden 十七歲少女湖: the mist passing over the valley’s surface like being chased by the morning light, casting shadows on the lake like waves.
To me, it was the nature’s artwork. No words can describe what I saw unless you get there to see it yourself.
When I stood by the ponds, the reflection of the sky, mist, fog, and water made me confused all of a sudden. I wasn’t sure where I was, whether I was floating in the air or still glued on the soil near the water and being embraced by the moss and mist. If there weren’t hikers taking photos by the water, I would believe I was in an up-side down world.

Off-putting Breakfast Queue

While I immersed myself totally in the dreamy surroundings, someone yelled at us to line up for breakfast at 6:30 am. I reluctantly pulled myself away and got back to the kitchen tent to queue up with other hikers. While standing in a line, someone flew a drone which caused hikers in front of my overexcitedly jumping up and down, trying to draw the attention. I had to turn away to hide my rolling eyes from them and looked at the lake, and I was struck by the view again.
When I thought I had seen the most majestic light at Lake Songluo, she decided to catch me off guard again. She stirred the light and mist up once more and set off another round of wonder. Seeing this, I really wanted to abandon my breakfast to embrace the amazing phenomenon again, but I couldn’t. If I missed breakfast now and came back later, I might delay our group’s departure. As a result, I could only watch the marvelous show with those ugly tents in front of me. I could feel my heart was feeling excited but also weeping at the same time.

A Surprisingly Beautiful Woods and River Nanshi 南勢溪

Around 7:10 am, our guide gathered us together again and we were ready to for the trip to River Nanshi. Usually, most group would head to River Nanshi to fetch fresh water after they arrived at Lake Songluo. I think our porters just took the water from the small ponds near our campsite. There were too many hikers to fetch enough water. I wouldn’t expect them to filter the water, either.
The trail to River Nanshi was very short and easy to hike, but the woods was a great delight. Ferns and usnea were almost everywhere. The terrains were muddier, too. I wore a pair of rainboots, so it was not a problem for me. I wish I could stay here much longer to explore this beautiful wood, but we had to get hurry.
After making a left turn, we arrived at River Nanshi. The water was so fresh, and the trees were tall and vibrant. Compared to the crowded Lake Songluo, this place was even better and much quieter. I walked around, checking trees here and there, trying to think whether this was a good place to pitch tents.
The weather was so great in the early morning. The sunlight just went through the ferns and usnea hanging on the trees. Perhaps this part of River Nanshi wasn’t as appealing as Lake Songluo, but for people who prefer being left alone like me, this was a heaven.
After breakfast, we gathered our backpacks and things together and ready to get back to our bus. Since this was a point-to-point trail, we took the same way back.

Rogue Hikers and Lousy Behaviors

If you have read this far, you probably can detect a hint of resentment in my writing. Please don’t get me wrong. I love this trail. The nature is amazing. What makes me upset is the hikers’ behaviors.
Lake Songluo has been famous among hikers in Taiwan for years. Therefore, many businesses rely on this trail to earn a living, which means it’s very easy to hire porters to carry tents, sleeping bags and food for hikers. However, during Covid-19, most people including myself don’t feel safe to travel abroad. The domestic travel, especially those destinations in the nature, becomes extremely popular.
I'm happy to see so many people getting close to the nature, but what they have done isn’t showing any love to the nature at all. Taiwan is a beautiful place, but its beauty is tainted by our lousy behaviors: toilet papers, plastic bottles, booming music, loud chatting and laughers, etc. When it rained in the late evening, I thought I finally could get a quiet night, but I was wrong. The rain didn’t stop hikers’ loud annoying chats.
Not to mention some hikers used the not so much pond water to wash their utensils. My neighbor told me he saw a film of grease floating on the top of the pond. I saw at least two hikers brushing their teeth with toothpastes. Did they swallow the toothpaste and water? Of course not.
We've done impact on those fragile ecosystem already. The least we can do is Leave No Trace, please.

No Respect to Other People’s Privacy

I brought a mesh tent with a tarp. I should have set the mesh opening toward the other side, because too many hikers with excess curiosity just peeked into my tent when they passed by. Some even thought it was totally fine to check on me with their headlamps. Taking a mesh tent doesn’t mean you are welcome to look inside with your headlamp, people.
One thing I don’t understand is we have to apply for permit to enter Lake Songluo but I don’t think there is a total amount control. Some hikers don’t want to carry heavy backpack, so they make the hike in one day.

Some Thoughts about This Hike

Will I come back to visit this beautiful lake? I’d love to, but I definitely will do things differently.
First, I will visit here in spring or fall. It's too hot and humid to hike the trail in summer, especially when you want to carry your own food, tent, and other gear.
Second, I will avoid going there on the weekend. You’ve seen those photos of tent city, so I assume there’s no need for me to explain more.
Third, I'll bring my own food, tent, etc., and pitch my tent far away from other hikers if possible. And I will not set my mesh tent opening toward people. I will also hike further to River Nanshi and fetch water there.
If you get a chance to visit Lake Songluo, I hope you will not see too many ugly things on the trail and can purely enjoy stunning views at Lake Songluo, and the vibrant ecosystem. If you are lucky enough, you might be able to have a starry sky and even shooting stars to tuck you in bed.
